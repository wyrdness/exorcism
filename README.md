## Overview

Exorcism is a religious or spiritual practice to expel demons, spirits, or other entities from individuals believed to be possessed. Practiced across multiple faith traditions including Catholic, Protestant, Islamic, Hindu, Buddhist, and Jewish variations.

**Major traditions**:
- **Catholic**: Formal Rite of Exorcism, authorized priest, bishop permission required
- **Protestant**: Deliverance ministries (varies by denomination)
- **Islamic**: Ruqya (Qur'an recitation and prayers)
- **Hindu/Buddhist/Jewish**: Various traditional rituals

**Famous exorcists**: Father Gabriele Amorth (claimed 60,000+ exorcisms)

**Famous cases**: 
- Anneliese Michel (1976) â€” Died during exorcism; criminal trial
- Roland Doe (1949) â€” Inspired *The Exorcist*

**Modern Church position** (Catholic): Psychiatric evaluation required first; genuine possession extremely rare; cautious approach.

**Dangers**: Documented deaths, delayed psychiatric treatment, physical abuse

**Scientific view**: No evidence of demon expulsion; ritual may provide psychological comfort (placebo), but psychiatric treatment more effective

## Major Traditions & Procedures

### 1. Catholic Christianity

**Formal Name**: Rite of Exorcism (Rituale Romanum)

**Authorization**: Requires bishop permission

**Practitioner**: Authorized priest (exorcist)

**Requirements**:
- âœ… Psychiatric evaluation **first**
- âœ… Evidence of genuine possession (not mental illness)
- âœ… Bishop authorization
- âœ… Multiple sessions often required

**Procedure**:
1. Prayers (Litany of Saints)
2. Gospel readings
3. Commands to demon to depart
4. Use of holy water
5. Display of crucifix
6. Laying on of hands

**Current Status**: Active; Church continues to train exorcists

---

### 2. Protestant Christianity

**Formal Name**: Deliverance Ministry

**Authorization**: Varies by denomination

**Practitioner**: Pastor or deliverance minister

**Requirements**: Varies widely (some require training, others do not)

**Procedure**:
- Prayer
- Commanding demons to leave in Jesus's name
- Varies significantly by denomination/group

**Current Status**: Active in some denominations (Pentecostal, Charismatic)

---

### 3. Islam

**Formal Name**: Ruqya

**Authorization**: Performed by knowledgeable Muslim

**Practitioner**: Scholar, imam, or knowledgeable person

**Requirements**:
- Knowledge of Qur'an and Islamic jurisprudence
- Proper intention (niyyah)
- Avoiding forbidden practices (shirk)

**Procedure**:
1. Recitation of specific Qur'anic verses
2. Supplications (du'a)
3. Blowing on person or water
4. Use of blessed water

**Current Status**: Widely practiced in Islamic world

---

### 4. Hinduism

**Formal Name**: Various rituals (vary by region)

**Practitioner**: Priest, tantrik, or ritual specialist

**Procedure**:
- Mantras
- Offerings
- Ritual cleansing
- Invocation of deities
- Varies significantly by region and tradition

**Current Status**: Active in traditional contexts

---

### 5. Buddhism

**Formal Name**: Various rituals

**Practitioner**: Monk or lama

**Procedure**:
- Chanting of sutras
- Meditation
- Use of ritual objects
- Varies by tradition (Theravada, Mahayana, Tibetan)

**Current Status**: Active in some Buddhist cultures

---

### 6. Judaism

**Formal Name**: Exorcism of dibbukim (possessing spirits)

**Practitioner**: Rabbi (historically)

**Procedure**:
- Prayers
- Psalms
- Hebrew incantations
- Blowing of shofar

**Current Status**: Historically practiced; very rare today

## Famous Cases

### Anneliese Michel (1975-1976) âš ï¸

> **Date**: 1975-1976 (67 sessions over 10 months)
> **Location**: Klingenberg, Bavaria, Germany
> **Tradition**: Catholic
> **Outcome**: Death; criminal convictions

**Background**:
- Young woman with epilepsy and psychiatric conditions
- Ceased medical treatment
- Underwent 67 exorcism sessions

**What Happened**:
- Died of malnutrition and dehydration
- Priests and parents convicted of negligent homicide
- Major international controversy

**Significance**: **Demonstrates extreme danger of exorcism when psychiatric treatment is needed**

## Scientific Perspective

### Scientific Consensus

**No evidence that exorcism expels actual demons or spirits**

### How Exorcism May Seem to "Work"

#### 1. Placebo Effect
Ritual provides psychological relief; expectation of cure creates improvement

#### 2. Social/Family Support
Attention and support from community provides comfort

#### 3. Natural Course of Illness
Some psychiatric conditions improve over time regardless of intervention

#### 4. Psychiatric Treatment More Effective
Actual psychiatric care addresses underlying conditions

### Medical Consensus

**Psychiatric conditions require psychiatric treatment.** Exorcism may provide cultural/religious comfort but is **not a substitute for medical care**.

## Modern Practice

### Catholic Church Current Position

1. **Psychiatric evaluation required** before exorcism considered
2. Genuine possession considered **extremely rare**
3. Medical treatment continues alongside any spiritual intervention
4. Only authorized priests may perform major exorcism
5. Bishop permission mandatory
6. Cautious, careful approach

### Reported Trends

- Some reports of **increased requests** for exorcism in 21st century
- Church continues **training exorcists** (courses in Rome and elsewhere)
- Emphasis on **discernment** and **caution**
